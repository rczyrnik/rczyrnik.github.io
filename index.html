Introduction



Hello! I am a data scientist studing at the Galvanize intensive program in San Francisco. As a Connecticut native, I'm also settling into my first snow-free winter, and learning to apreciate t-shirt weather in November.

I put together this webpage to practice HTML and CSS. I know it may be simple compared to profesionally-designed sites, but it was made from scratch with love. It is both a project and a repository for my projects, which is pretty meta! I hope you like it.

In my previous life I was a high school physics teacher, and my experiences in the classroom continue to inform my new life in data science. Most directly, years in the classroom developed a passion in me for explaining difficult concepts in a clear and simple manner to an audience with a wide range of backgrounds. I look forward to transfering these skills to my work in data science, explaining results to people both within and outside my specialty.

I'd love to hear about what you like and don't like. Presumably if you're reading this it's because you already know me, so send me a line in the usual way! If you don't know me, then what brought you here? I want to know! You can find me at rczyrnik@gmail.com

Calculators

I like calculators, especially my trusty TI-89 which has carried me through thick and thin. But the calculators I use, like the TI-89 are really complicated, and I wondered what they would look like stripped down to their most basic parts.

Camera

Continuing with the theme of creating for myself crude versions of technology already perfected by the professionals, I built a black-and-white camera. I used photo-sensitive paper to take a picture of the brightest object I could think of, the sun.

Dog Games

I often feel bad that I'm not properly educating my dog, teaching him letters and numbers and shapes and colors. Hopefully this app will change that! At the moment, the game I developed dispenses trests when the dog/cat/wereworlf hits the blue button on an iDevice's screen.

Cell Phone Spectroscope

Our cellphone cameras capture images using technology very similar to that found in space telescopes. This journey began with an attempt to plot the black body radiation from our sun and will hopefully end with a program that identifies a gas based on its spectral lines.
