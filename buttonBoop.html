Introduction

Among my dog's favorite activites is lounging in bed all day while I'm at work. I'd rather he get a little bit of activity while he's home alone so I built a game that he can play on his own.

The game has two parts: a Swift program that runs on an iPad, and an Arduino robot that dispenses kibble when triggered by the Swift program. You can see it in action below:




The Game


Front Screen
At the moment the program has two modes, but I hope to add more interesting challenges this summer.


Blue Boop
The dog/cat/velociraptor wins by pressing anywhere on the screen. It is not a very dificult game.


Button Boop
The animal/small child wins by pressing the blue half of the screen. The program keeps track of "on target" and "off target" presses.

Next Steps

The program is still in its infancy and there are a lot of improvements I want to make

  1. Add a dog-proof way to exit the game, like a twisting gesture, so he can't accidentally hit the exit button
  2. Keep track of data and display progress in a chart
  3. Add dificulty settlings that change the size of the box or move its location
  4. Introduce distractions like other colored boxes, or blue circles and triangles
  5. Create a new game that rewards the dog for picking the right letter, or hitting boxes in the right order

Learning Swift

This was my first iOS project so there was a very steep learning curve. Xcode, the IDE for designing iOS apps, as well as Swift, the programming language, were not very learner-friendly. A lot went into every small step, from getting a sound to play, to switching into and out of the games, to just getting the program to run on my phone. And getting it onto the iPad was another adventure.

Despite the high levels of frustruation, I'm glad I undertook this project. The skills to program in Swift are very different from programing in Python, Ruby, Javascript, Fortran, and every other language I've studied. Knowing variable types and integers was helpful, by more important was patinece with Google searches, and a willingness to accept that I just won't understand what's going on for the time being, and that's okay. I don't understand even half of what everything means, but I'm making it work for me and that's something.

Plus, this is the first useful program I've made. It's rewarding to see Gandalf get excited when I show him the blue screen, and also to see his sad face when he realizes play time is over.
